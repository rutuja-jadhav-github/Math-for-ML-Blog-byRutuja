Before we begin, it's worth making explicit a few assumptions that quietly sit underneath both maximum likelihood estimation and machine learning loss minimization.

## I. Assumptions

**IID (Independent and Identically Distributed).** Most statistical learning theory assumes our observations are IID. *Independent* means each observation contributes genuinely new information, while *identically distributed* means all observations come from the same underlying population. For example, if you survey 1,000 people but 500 belong to just 100 families, knowing something about one family member tells you something about another—the observations are no longer truly independent. Similarly, if half your salary data comes from 2010 and half from 2024, the observations are not identically distributed because they are drawn from different populations.

**The Law of Large Numbers (LLN).** The practical importance of IID is that it allows the Law of Large Numbers to apply. As the sample size grows, quantities computed on the training set converge to their true population values. In machine learning, this is the bridge between training loss and real-world performance: minimizing empirical loss becomes a reasonable proxy for minimizing the loss we would observe on unseen data drawn from the same population.

**Maximum Likelihood and Loss Minimization.** At first glance, MLE and machine learning seem to solve different problems. MLE asks: *which parameters make the observed data most probable?* Machine learning asks: *which parameters minimize prediction error?* Under common probabilistic assumptions, these turn out to be the same optimization problem expressed in different language. The loss functions we optimize are often nothing more than negative log-likelihoods in disguise.

**No systematic bias in the errors.** We assume that after accounting for the observed features, the remaining noise is just random noise — it does not consistently push our estimates in one direction or another.

In other words, our estimate $\hat\theta$ is centered around the true parameter and is therefore an unbiased estimator of $\theta$. If we repeatedly collected new datasets and refit the model, the estimates would fluctuate due to randomness, but they would not be systematically too large or too small.

Mathematically, this assumption is written as:

$$
E[\varepsilon \mid X] = 0.
$$

Intuitively, this means there are no important hidden factors lurking inside the error term that are correlated with our features which would otherwise generate a systematically biased $\hat\theta$.

**Gaussian Errors.** In linear regression, we assume the error term is normally distributed. This assumption does more than describe noise—it determines the likelihood function itself. At any fixed value of (x), the model predicts a mean response, while actual observations vary around that mean according to a Gaussian distribution with constant variance (homoskedasticity!). As we will see shortly, once Gaussian errors are assumed, maximizing likelihood naturally leads to the squared-error objective used in ordinary least squares regression.

Key takeaway is that as long as you have a large number of iid samples allowing LLN and CLT to kick in, Gaussian errors and homoskedasticity are not absolutely essentially for unbiasedness. Assuming that that erros are Gaussian does however directly result in least squares emerging as the loss function, which is the crux of this derivation.


## II. Preliminary Probabilistic Modelling


Our derivation actually begins from that last assumption. We have gaussian errors:

$$
\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)
$$

Since adding a constant to a Gaussian random variable simply shifts its centre without changing its shape, the distribution of the outcome variable is also Gaussian:

$$
y_i \mid x_i \sim \mathcal{N}(x_i^\top \theta,\sigma^2)
$$

**Note - the assumption is NOT simply:**

$$
y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(x_i^\top \theta,\sigma^2)
$$

**This distinction matters because the model is not claiming that all salaries, house prices, or outcomes in the dataset come from a single Gaussian distribution. Rather, it claims that for a fixed set of features, the possible outcomes are distributed normally around the model's prediction.** Once we fix a particular value of $x_i$, the model’s prediction $x_i^\top \theta$ is no longer random—it becomes a single number. The only remaining source of randomness is the error term $\varepsilon_i$.

A simple example is predicting salary from age. Suppose the model predicts a salary of USD 70,000 for a 30-year-old. Not every 30-year-old earns exactly USD 70,000—some earn more, others earn less. The assumption is that these possible outcomes are distributed normally around the model's prediction, with USD 70,000 sitting at the centre of the bell curve. In other words, the model is not predicting a single outcome; it is predicting the mean of a distribution of possible outcomes.

In a Gaussian distribution, the behavior of the data is controlled by two parameters: a mean and a variance. The mean determines the center of the distribution, while the variance controls how spread out the observations are around that center.

In the regression setting, the variance $\sigma^2$ is assumed to be constant (it does not depend on $x_i$). The mean, however, is not fixed. Instead, it is determined by the model and depends on the input features $x_i$ through the parameters $\theta$.

So we can think of:

* $\theta$: the parameters of the regression model (i.e, the slopes or coefficients, what we are trying to learn)
* $\mu_i$: the mean of the Gaussian for the $i$-th observation
* $\sigma^2$: the shared variance of the noise (assumed constant across all data points)

The key link we will use next is that the mean is defined by the model itself:

$$
\mu_i = x_i^\top \theta
$$

This is what connects linear regression to probability: the model does not directly predict $y_i$, but instead predicts the mean of a Gaussian distribution from which $y_i$ is assumed to be generated.

For a Gaussian distribution with mean $\mu_i$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the probability density of observing a single output $y_i$ given an input $x_i$ is:

$$
P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(y_i - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

Substituting $\mu_i = x_i^\top \theta$, this becomes:

$$
P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

At this stage, we are no longer describing a deterministic prediction. Instead, the model defines a probability distribution over possible outcomes $y_i$, centered at $x_i^\top \theta$, with variability controlled by $\sigma^2$. 

Think of a standard 2D $x$–$y$ plane, with $x$ on the horizontal axis and $y$ on the vertical axis.

Now fix a particular value of $x_i$. At that vertical slice, instead of a single point, imagine a full Gaussian distribution along the $y$-axis. It looks like a vertical bell curve centered at $x_i^\top \theta$, with spread $\sigma^2$.

Now repeat this for every $x_i$: at each position on the $x$-axis, there is a vertical bell curve stacked in the $y$ direction. The centers of all these curves lie on the straight line $x_i^\top \theta$.

So visually, the regression line is the curve passing through the centers of these vertical Gaussian slices across the plane.

## III. From Probability Distribution to Likelihood

So far, we have been thinking in terms of probability: given a fixed $\theta$, $x_i$, and $\sigma^2$, what is the probability of observing a particular $y_i$?

Now we change our perspective slightly.

We assume the dataset ${y_1, \dots, y_n}$ is already observed, and ask: *which value of $\theta$ makes this entire dataset most plausible?* This is the shift from probability to likelihood.

A key difference is conceptual: probabilities over outcomes sum to 1 under a fixed model. Likelihoods do not. Here, each choice of $\theta$ defines a different “universe,” so there is no requirement that values across different $\theta$ sum to 1.

We therefore define the likelihood of $\theta$ as the joint probability of observing all outputs given all inputs:

$X = {x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n}$ is the full set of inputs
$Y = {y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n}$ is the full set of outputs

So the likelihood is:


$$
L(\theta) = P\big(\{Y_1 = y_1\} \cap \{Y_2 = y_2\} \cap \cdots \cap \{Y_n = y_n\} \mid X, \theta \big)
$$

This gives us the probability that all outputs occur together, given the entire input dataset $X$ and parameters $\theta$.

And with our new perspective: we stop thinking of this as a probability of the data, and instead view it as a function of $\theta$. This function is the likelihood.

Expanding this directly would typically require the chain rule. However, under the IID assumption, the observations are independent given $X$ and $\theta$, so the joint probability factorizes into a product of per-data-point terms:

$$
L(\theta) = P(y_1 \mid x_1, \theta) \times P(y_2 \mid x_2, \theta) \times \cdots \times P(y_n \mid x_n, \theta)
$$



$$
L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta)
$$

So instead of reasoning about one complicated joint event, we now evaluate a product of individual Gaussian probabilities, one for each data point.



## IV. Log of Likelihood

Taking the likelihood function, we now apply a logarithm to convert the product into a sum. (Why do we do this? Because a little birdie told us we'll be working with derivatives later, and it is easier to differentiate sums over products.)

$$
\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta)
$$

Substituting the product form of the likelihood:

$$
\ell(\theta) = \log \prod_{i=1}^n P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta)
$$

Using the log rule $\log \prod = \sum \log$, we get:

$$
\ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta)
$$

Now substitute the Gaussian form of each conditional probability:

$$
P(y_i \mid x_i, \theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

So:

$$
\ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log \left[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
\exp\left(-\frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\right]
$$

Now split the log over multiplication:

$$
\ell(\theta) =
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left[
\log \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
+
\log \exp\left(-\frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
\right]
$$

Finally simplify the exponential term using $\log \exp(a) = a$:

$$
\ell(\theta) =
\sum_{i=1}^n
\log \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
-
\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}
$$

We first rewrite the log-likelihood expression:

$$
\ell(\theta)
=
\sum_{i=1}^n \log \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
-
\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}
$$

Now simplify each term.

The log constant term becomes:

$$
\log \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
= -\frac{1}{2} \log(2\pi\sigma^2)
$$

So the first summation becomes:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n \log \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
=
-\frac{n}{2} \log(2\pi\sigma^2)
$$

For the second term, factor out $\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}$:

$$
\ell(\theta)
=
-\frac{n}{2} \log(2\pi\sigma^2)
-
\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
$$


## V. Optmization: From MLE to Squared Loss

This is a good moment to pause and remember the original question. we are trying to find the value of $\theta$ that makes the observed data most plausible. Everything so far was just algebraic unpacking of one idea: which $\theta$ best explains the data we already observed? This is what we now formalise as an optimization problem by choosing the $\theta$ that maximises the log-likelihood:

$$
\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \ \ell(\theta)
$$

Substituting the expression above:

$$
\theta^*
=
\arg\max_\theta
\left[
-\frac{n}{2} \log(2\pi\sigma^2)
-
\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
\right]
$$

Now let’s zoom into the two terms inside the objective.

The first term:
$$
-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)
$$
is negative (since $\log(2\pi\sigma^2) > 0$), but more importantly it is a constant with respect to $\theta$. It does not change when we vary $\theta$.

The second term:
$$
-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
$$
is also negative, since it is the negative of a sum of squared errors multiplied by a positive constant.

**Intuitively, this makes sense because $\ell(\theta)$ is a log-likelihood. A likelihood is always between 0 and 1 (even though it does not sum to 1 across $\theta$), and the logarithm of any number in $(0,1]$ is always non-positive. Specifically, $\log(1) = 0$ and as the likelihood approaches 0, the log-likelihood goes to $-\infty$.**

In other words, **higher likelihood values correspond to log-likelihood values closer to 0, while lower likelihood values push $\ell(\theta)$ further into the negative.** We will connect this directly to loss functions shortly, but for now this tells us what **kind of objective we are dealing with: a quantity that is always negative, and is maximised when it is closest to zero.**

So the full objective has the structure:
$$
(-\text{constant}) - A(\theta)
$$

In other words, we are trying to maximise a negative quantity of the form $-A(\theta)$.

**Maximising a negative function is equivalent to minimising its magnitude, i.e. making $A(\theta)$ as small as possible.**

So instead of maximising the log-likelihood, we can equivalently solve:
$$
\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
$$

$$
\theta^*
= \arg\max_\theta \ell(\theta)
= \arg\max_\theta \left[
-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)
-
\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
\right]
= \arg\min_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2
$$


In other words, the optimal parameter $\theta^*$ is the one that best explains the data we actually observed.

But what is this term  $\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2$ actually doing?

Think about it visually — for each data point, we take the difference between what our model predicts ($x_i^\top \theta$) and what we actually observed ($y_i$).

Then we square that gap so positives and negatives don’t cancel out, and we penalise big mistakes more heavily.

Now we add all of those squared gaps up across the dataset.

Sound familiar? It’s exactly the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) loss — just written in probability clothing.

This can be expressed altogether as:


$$
\begin{aligned}
\theta^*
&= \text{parameter that maximises probability of observed data given } X \\
&= \arg\max_\theta P(Y \mid X, \theta) \\
&= \arg\min_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - x_i^\top \theta)^2 \\
&= \text{parameter that minimises prediction error between } x_i^\top \theta \text{ and } y_i
\end{aligned}
$$

The same $\theta$ can be viewed in two equivalent ways:
- it maximises the likelihood of observing the training data under a probabilistic model  
- it minimises the squared difference between predictions $x_i^\top \theta$ and actual outcomes $y_i$

So the conclusion is:

**In maximum likelihood estimation, we maximize the probability of observing the data under a model. In machine learning, we minimize a loss function.** These are the same operation written in different languages.

**The ML loss is simply the negative log-likelihood.**

And empirical risk minimization **(ERM) is nothing more than maximum likelihood estimation expressed without probabilistic framing.**


## VI. Optimal $\theta$ and Minmizing the Squared Loss

In summary, finding the optimal $\theta$ means minimising this squared error.

The actual optimisation step comes from differentiation — which is what turns this from an idea into a solution.

If you work through that (in the next post), you eventually arrive at:

$$
\theta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y
$$

where:
- $\theta$ is the vector of model parameters (weights/ slopes/ coefficients we want to learn)  
- $X$ is the design matrix of input features (should always be full rank - shall decode this in another post)
- $y$ is the vector of observed outputs (targets)
